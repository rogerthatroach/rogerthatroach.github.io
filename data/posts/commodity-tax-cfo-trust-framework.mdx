In September 2022 I joined <Glossed>CFO Group</Glossed> at RBC as a Senior Data Scientist. Nobody had asked me to build AI. I was hired to help automate the <Glossed>Commodity Tax</Glossed> return process — a manual workflow that consumed finance analysts for months per cycle.

By Q4 2023, the process took **90 minutes**. The metric earned the **CFO Group RBC Quarterly Team Award**. More importantly, it earned the credibility to propose Aegis, Astraeus, and eventually Prometheus — a cascade that shaped the next three years.

This post walks the architectural decisions that made it land — under explicit constraints, against named alternatives, with the reasoning that produced each call. The narrative version of this story (with the cycle-1 anchor moment that compressed the whole strategy into a single afternoon) lives in the [builder companion](/blog/commodity-tax-cfo-trust). The formal frame — pipeline as a sequence of provenance-preserving graph rewrites, Tableau as a parallel presentation layer derived from the same provenance algebra — lives in the [formal post](/blog/commodity-tax-provenance).

## Constraints before choices

Five constraints shaped every decision below. Three regulatory or hard-technical, two soft-organizational. The Tableau-as-transparency-layer architecture is the only option that satisfies all five simultaneously.

## First project, first audition

When you join an organization as the AI person and your first assignment is process automation, there are two ways to read it. You can treat it as a detour — &ldquo;I&rsquo;ll do this so I can get to the real AI work.&rdquo; Or you can treat it as an audition.

I treated it as an audition. Finance leaders were not going to green-light a machine-learning platform for CFO-level decisions on the strength of my CV. They were going to green-light it on the strength of my first delivery.

That framing changed how I built.

## Two architectural decisions

The first decision was close to obvious. The second decision is the whole post.

This is the decision that built trust.

(The <a href="/blog/commodity-tax-provenance">formal post</a> develops this as the provenance-algebra theorem; here it&rsquo;s enough to know that building both surfaces from one substrate is cheaper than building either alone.)

## What this opened

By the time Commodity Tax was running cleanly, the permission structure inside the CFO Group had shifted. I could propose <Glossed>Aegis v1</Glossed> — a benchmarking engine for the Big 6 Canadian banks — and get the air cover to build it. When Aegis v1 shipped (2025 CFO One RBC Team Award), I could propose <Glossed>Aegis v2</Glossed>, the text-to-SQL refactor, and deliver it in a concurrent 2-week sprint while Astraeus productionization was underway. When <Glossed>Astraeus</Glossed> reached production scale, I had the credibility to conceive <Glossed>Prometheus</Glossed>, hand the concept to the 2025 Amplify cohort for problem-space ideation, and then build the bank-wide platform end-to-end.

Every one of those projects was technically ambitious. None of them would have been greenlit by a new hire with nothing to show for it. Commodity Tax was underwriting every subsequent bet.

## What I&rsquo;d do differently

One scope call I&rsquo;d revisit: the first dashboard set surfaced provenance for *outputs* but not for *rules*. Analysts could drill from a derived number back to the source GL rows, but the rules that produced each category mapping lived in PySpark code, modifiable only by the data team. New tax categories or re-categorizations of existing GL accounts therefore required code changes and redeploys.

In retrospect I&rsquo;d have built a *mapping-rules dashboard* from cycle 2 onward — letting analysts propose rule additions directly (still requiring code-review approval before production), turning the mapping layer from a single-person bottleneck into a shared surface. Formally (see the [provenance post](/blog/commodity-tax-provenance) §7), this is a missing presentation function: a <em>π<sub>ρ</sub></em> over the rule set itself, not just <em>π<sub>i</sub></em> over the data states. The transparency-as-architecture principle worked for outputs; I should have applied it to the rules at the same time. It would have cost about a week in v1 and saved much more than that across the next two years.

## The pattern

Your first project at a new organization isn&rsquo;t just a deliverable. It&rsquo;s the audition that determines which doors open next.

The architectural decision — Tableau as a transparency layer, not an output — was the first time I applied a principle I&rsquo;d use in every subsequent system: **the hardest thing in production AI isn&rsquo;t getting the answer right; it&rsquo;s getting the humans who own the decision to trust the system that produced it.**

Astraeus&rsquo;s LLM-as-Router separation is a descendant of this idea. Aegis v2&rsquo;s confidence-thresholded clarification is a descendant of this idea. Prometheus&rsquo;s typed MCP tool contracts that log every field assignment are a descendant of this idea. In every production AI system at a regulated institution, the architecture has to survive stakeholder audit.

Commodity Tax was where I learned that. Cycle 1 was where the lesson actually landed. The technology changes. The pattern doesn&rsquo;t.
