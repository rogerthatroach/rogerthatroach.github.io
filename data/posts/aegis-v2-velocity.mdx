Aegis v2 went from concept to production in two weeks. I was simultaneously running Astraeus development and the 2025 summer intern program. The product shipped anyway. It earned the 2025 CFO One RBC Team Award.

This isn&rsquo;t a post about working harder. It&rsquo;s a post about what velocity actually looks like when the architecture is right.

## The problem

RBC&rsquo;s CFO Group benchmarks financial KPIs against the Big 6 Canadian banks using the Supplementary Financial Packages each bank publishes with its quarterly earnings. Publicly available, but complex &mdash; every bank names and nests its KPIs differently, CET1 Ratios live in different tables, similar-sounding metrics mean subtly different things. Finance analysts did this benchmarking by hand: opening each bank&rsquo;s SFP PDF, hunting for the right number, transcribing into a spreadsheet, reconciling, repeating every quarter. Hours per query.

Aegis v1 was a rules-based benchmarking engine I&rsquo;d already built and productionized. It handled a defined catalog of KPIs well, but it couldn&rsquo;t accept natural language. If an analyst wanted to ask "how does our cost-to-income ratio compare against the peer average this quarter?" they still had to translate that question into v1&rsquo;s query syntax, or go back to the spreadsheet.

Aegis v2 was the AI-native evolution: natural language in, validated SQL out, with embedding-based KPI disambiguation and architectural safety guarantees the analysts could actually trust.

## The five-stage pipeline

The spine of v2 is a decomposed pipeline where each stage owns one concern:

1. **Intent parsing** &mdash; rule-based. Decomposes the natural-language query into structured components: which KPIs, which banks, which time window, which comparison operator.
2. **KPI detection** &mdash; deterministic. Maps query terms to a metadata-rich KPI catalog derived from the SFPs.
3. **Disambiguation** &mdash; embeddings-based similarity search, LLM-assisted only when confidence thresholds say the deterministic match is ambiguous. "CET1 Ratio" and "Common Equity Tier 1 Capital Ratio" resolve to the same thing; "Operating Leverage" across two banks might resolve to different underlying calculations, and the system has to know when to ask.
4. **SQL generation** &mdash; guardrailed. Whitelisted schema, parameterized queries, forbidden-keyword filter. SQL injection is impossible by construction, not "unlikely in practice."
5. **Deterministic formatting** &mdash; output is structured and identical for identical inputs.

The formal paper proves the safety properties. What matters here is that each stage is independently testable and each stage fails in a way the next stage can handle. No stage is load-bearing on LLM magic. The LLM does what language models do well (disambiguation under uncertainty), bounded by confidence thresholds. Everything else is code.

## What two weeks actually requires

The "two weeks" metric is real. What&rsquo;s misleading about it is the framing that implies the work happened in that window.

The work happened in the months before. What happened during the sprint was crystallization, not improvisation.

1. **The architecture was already rehearsed.** I&rsquo;d been living with v1 in production for a year. I knew its failure modes: where the rules engine broke, which KPI categories were most ambiguous, what analysts actually asked for vs. what v1 could answer. That dataset-of-one-year-of-operating-knowledge was the specification for v2. When the sprint started, I wasn&rsquo;t designing. I was implementing a design I&rsquo;d already committed to in principle.

2. **The pipeline was decomposable.** Five stages with clear contracts meant I could build and test each one independently. Intent parsing on day one. KPI catalog loading on day two. Disambiguation harness on day three. Etc. No single stage needed to be right before the next could start. At any given moment, two or three stages were in flight in parallel in my head. That kind of decomposition is an architectural property; it&rsquo;s also an execution property. It makes speed possible because it makes work parallelizable.

3. **Scope was ruthlessly bounded.** v2 did not do a lot of things. It didn&rsquo;t try to be a universal financial QA system. It didn&rsquo;t support free-form numerical reasoning. It didn&rsquo;t rewrite the visualization layer. Every temptation to expand scope during the sprint got deferred to post-launch. "We&rsquo;ll see if they ask for it once it&rsquo;s in production" is a harder answer to give than "sure, I&rsquo;ll add that," but it&rsquo;s the answer that keeps the ship date real.

Underneath all three, there was also capacity protection. Astraeus was mid-flight. The summer intern program had interns I was mentoring. Two weeks of Aegis v2 work was possible only because the architecture&rsquo;s clarity meant I didn&rsquo;t need to be in the room for most implementation decisions. I could move between workstreams without each one losing momentum.

## The result

- Shipped to production in 2 weeks
- Natural-language KPI benchmarking replacing manual Supplementary Financial Package lookups
- 2025 CFO One RBC Team Award
- Aegis v1, the rules-based predecessor, continued running in production through the rewrite &mdash; zero downtime on the benchmarking function that analysts relied on

## The pattern

Two ideas worth taking seriously:

1. **Velocity is a downstream effect of clarity.** The work that makes a two-week sprint possible is the research, prototyping, and operational exposure that preceded it. If you&rsquo;re trying to compress novel architectural work into two weeks, you&rsquo;re not doing it in two weeks. If you&rsquo;re implementing a design you&rsquo;ve already validated in production, two weeks is realistic.

2. **Decomposable pipelines beat monoliths.** Not only for testability, not only for maintainability &mdash; also for speed. Clear stage contracts let you parallelize work in a way monolithic agent architectures cannot.

Aegis v2 is structurally a descendant of Astraeus&rsquo;s LLM-as-Router &mdash; LLMs used surgically, deterministic code handling everything else, with the LLM&rsquo;s role bounded by schemas and confidence thresholds. The systems look different because they&rsquo;re in different domains. Architecturally they&rsquo;re the same family.

The hardest part wasn&rsquo;t shipping fast. The hardest part was knowing what not to build.
